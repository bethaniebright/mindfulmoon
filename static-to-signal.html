You would tell someone what's wrong if you knew.

Something is off and you can't put your finger on it. You snap at people who didn't deserve it and feel bad about it for three days. You cry at commercials and stay dry-eyed at things that actually matter. You're tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix, or you're wired and can't sit still, or both in the same afternoon.

If you could name it, you could do something about it. But you can't, so you carry it around and hope it works itself out, and it doesn't.

You're not going to think your way to the answer. I spent years trying.

You don't have to know what it is to start. Thirty days of making art, one prompt a day, and it shows up on the page before you can explain it.

You are carrying something you can't see.

It doesn't announce itself. It comes out sideways.

You lie awake at one in the morning with a brain that won't shut off. Your body hurts and the tests come back normal. You've been asked what's wrong so many times that you have a stock answer ready, and it isn't a lie exactly, it just isn't anything.

None of this means you're broken. You're carrying something and nobody ever gave you a way to put it down.

You can't say it. You can still make it.

To talk about something you have to know what it is first, then find the word for it, then say the word out loud to a person who's going to have a reaction. That's a lot of steps and you can't even do the first one.

The page doesn't ask you for any of that.

Scribble red across it when you don't know why you're angry. Rip something up. Cover a page in black and leave three words showing.

You don't have to understand it. You just have to make it. Then you look at what you made, and sometimes it tells you something you didn't know you knew.

This is what happened to me.

I got sober. I went back to school. I got diagnosed with an autoimmune disease and nobody could tell me why.

I started making art every day, not to heal anything, I just needed somewhere to put what I was feeling.

Things started coming up. Things I did not remember. Things I could not have told you were in there, because I genuinely did not know.

The art journal is where I found them. Not because I was brave and not because I was ready, but because I was making marks on a page every day and eventually the page told me the truth.

My autoimmune disease is in remission now.

Thirty prompts, in an order that matters.

One email every morning. Nothing to log into. It shows up, you make something. The order is the design — each week only works because of the one before it.

Week one

Tuning the antenna

You have to make a mark before you can make meaning. Week one is easy on purpose. You probably haven't made anything in years and the blank page is loud, so we start by getting you comfortable making noise on it. Nothing is asked of you except that you show up and put something down.

Week two

Finding your frequency

This is where it gets honest. You will have been making art for a week by then, and things start surfacing that you weren't expecting. Places you've said yes when you meant no. Doors you didn't walk through and never really looked at again. You didn't know that was in there. Neither did I. This is the week it starts working, and it's the week you'll want to quit, and those are the same thing.

Week three

From static to signal

The hard one. Resistance. Perfectionism. The feelings you've organized your life around not having, which you may not have realized you were organizing your life around until week two showed you. You put it on the page and find out you don't die.

Week four

Full spectrum

Making it yours, so it still works after I stop emailing you.

Every week starts with what happened to me in that territory and why those prompts are the ones I picked.

You fill out a questionnaire on day one and the same one on day thirty. Your stress, your energy, how many days your body felt off, how you talk to yourself. Nobody remembers how they felt a month ago. This way you don't have to.

It's not the time and it's not the supplies.

You're scared it's going to be bad, and that the badness will mean something about you.

Nobody is grading it. Nobody sees it unless you want them to. It can be ugly, ripped up, scribbled over, so bad you can't tell what it was supposed to be. All of it counts.

One rule: do not talk shit about your art. That's the whole rulebook.

Supplies don't matter. Printer paper, junk mail, a glue stick, pens that barely work. If you're waiting until you have the right art journal, you're not getting ready. You're avoiding. I've done it.

You're going to miss days. When it happens, don't go back to the beginning. The urge to start over is perfectionism in a fucking disguise, and the people who quit are the ones who missed three days and decided they'd ruined it.

Questions

How long does this take?

As long as you've got. Ten minutes is a real practice, and day one is one mark on a page.

What do I have to buy?

Nothing. Paper and a pen. There's an optional list inside but none of it makes this work better.

I'm not creative.

Everyone quoted on this page said that before they started.

What if I don't know what's bothering me?

That's who this is for. You don't need to know, and honestly you're not supposed to yet. That's the whole point of making it instead of saying it.

Is this going to bring stuff up?

Probably. Not in week one, but by week two things tend to surface that people weren't expecting, and that's the practice working rather than going wrong. If something comes up that an art journal can't hold, go talk to someone who can hold it with you.

Is this therapy?

No. I'm a licensed art therapist and this isn't clinical treatment.

Are you saying art cured your autoimmune disease?

I'm saying stress makes autoimmune symptoms worse, and making art every day brought my stress down further than anything else I tried. My disease is in remission. I'm telling you what happened to me, not what will happen to you.

What if I fall behind?

You will. Pick up where you are, don't start over.
